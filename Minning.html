Mapping Minning Areas using Machine Learning

Project Overview

The initial step in identifying the utilized mining area involves applying a machine learning algorithm, specifically the Random Forest model, trained with samples collected by the experts from the Geospatial Observation Team (EOG). Random Forest, a supervised classification model, is capable of processing large volumes of geospatial data and detecting complex patterns in imagery. Due to its ability to handle non-linear features and high-dimensional data, it is highly effective in identifying potential areas where mining activities take place with remarkable accuracy.

For this process, the Google Earth Engine platform is used, providing access to a vast array of satellite data and advanced geospatial processing tools. These capabilities allow us to complement the classification with additional information from various sources, such as radar data and digital terrain models, thereby enhancing the analysis and improving the accuracy of the results.

The samples used to train the algorithm are selected from areas known for mining activity, enabling the model to learn the specific characteristics that distinguish these areas from others. Once trained, the algorithm can efficiently classify new areas, accurately identifying those likely associated with mining activities.

Methodology

We employed satellite imagery (Planet) and geospatial data analysis techniques to identify utilized mining area. The key steps in the project include:

  • Selection and downloading of satellite images: Satellite images from Planet data are selected and downloaded for the analysis of mining areas.
  • Integration of additional data to improve accuracy: Additional data, such as digital terrain models and radar, are integrated to enhance the classification and improve its accuracy.
  • Application of the Random Forest classification algorithm: The Random Forest classification model is used to train with samples extracted from mining areas, enabling precise identification of mining zones.
  • Validation of supervised classification: An expert reviews the classified areas, correcting errors and adjusting the classification to ensure accuracy.
  • Use of high-resolution images for further verification: High-resolution images from SecureWatch are used for detailed verification and adjustment of identified mining areas.
  • Generation of results and final reports: A detailed report is generated with identified mining areas, including accuracy analysis and classification metrics.
  • Continuous update and monitoring: A monthly workflow is established to update the Used Mining Area using Planet data and new satellite images.

Results & Impact

As a result of this project, several deforested regions have been identified and mapped for reforestation. The key outcomes include:

  • Continuous Monitoring and Updates: The system is capable of updating the "Used Mining Area" on a monthly basis, ensuring that the data remains current and reflective of any new mining activity or changes. This dynamic approach allows for real-time monitoring and faster decision-making.
  • Improved Decision-Making: With a robust and accurate dataset of mining areas, stakeholders—including environmental authorities, mining regulators, and land management agencies—can make informed decisions regarding land use, environmental protection, and resource allocation.
  • Increased Efficiency in Monitoring: By automating the classification process and integrating additional data sources, the system reduces manual work, streamlining monitoring tasks and improving the efficiency of tracking mining activities.
